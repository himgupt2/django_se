Welcome

The individual animal’s experience is critical to assessing and understanding animal welfare.

Our research is aimed at quantifying the behavior of livestock that will provide producers with scientifically supported management strategies to enhance animal welfare and optimize productivity.

Current research interests:

  • Evaluate husbandry practices that will help animal managers utilize temperament and behavior to enhance productivity
  • Develop and validate technologies designed to biological events (e.g., illness, calving) in cattle and to characterize individual cattle behavior
  • Examine the impact of exercise and the influence of temperament on immune function and stress responsivity in receiving cattle exposed to common stressors (e.g., commingling, transportation)

What We Do

Dr. Courtney Daigle is assistant professor at the department of animal sciences at Texas A & M University. This website is an insight into our research and findings. You can reach me through my contact in human medicine. They have to identify and catalog a large library of these bacteriophage and in doing so, the team generates an immense amount of data that must be available and accessed in a reasonable way by their researchers.

Currently, this data is not centralized in a meaningful way and it is increasingly difficult to track where a given phage is in the identification and development process. As they expand the library of phage, this problem will grow to become more and more unmanageable. So a system like the proposed Phage DB project will allow them to store, categorize, and analyze the data more intelligently and easily. Providing an intuitive UI will allow their researchers to interact with the system more regularly and streamline the data storage, allowing more focus on the science over the bookkeeping.
